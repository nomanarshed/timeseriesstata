Time series analysis sits at the centre of applied macroeconomics, finance, and energy economics. Whenever a single entity is observed over time — a country's GDP, an exchange rate, a commodity price, a firm's returns — the time dimension brings its own set of problems: trends, unit roots, autocorrelation, seasonality, structural breaks, and changing volatility. Stata handles all of it, but the commands are spread across a wide methodological range, from simple regression to ARDL, causality testing, error correction, and GARCH.

This playlist organises the workflow into a sensible learning path. Work through the stages in order. Each one assumes the previous: you cannot interpret an ARDL bounds test without first getting the series imported and stationarity sorted, and the logic of error correction only makes sense once the regression and cointegration foundations are in place.

Stage 1

Foundations & Data Management

Every time series analysis is only as reliable as the series behind it. Before any model runs, the data must be brought in cleanly, declared to Stata as time series, transformed appropriately, and free of internal gaps. This stage covers the unglamorous but decisive groundwork — getting data out of Excel, deciding whether to work in levels or logs, and repairing missing observations without distorting the underlying dynamics.

Stage 2

Regression & ARDL Models

With clean, stationary-checked data in hand, this stage builds the core modelling toolkit. It starts with simple time series regression and the diagnostics that must accompany it, then moves into the ARDL family — the workhorse for series mixing I(0) and I(1) variables — and its more specialised variants for asymmetry and seasonality.

Stage 1 leaves you with a properly declared, gap-free series. From here, the question becomes how variables relate over time. ARDL is the natural starting framework because it tolerates mixed orders of integration and recovers both short-run and long-run effects in one specification — which is why so much applied time series work lives here.
04 / Stage 2

A comprehensive tutorial on running a basic time series regression and, crucially, performing the diagnostic tests that come with it — autocorrelation, heteroskedasticity, and stability checks. The diagnostics are not optional housekeeping; they determine whether the regression can be trusted at all.

Stage 3

Causality & Dynamics

Estimating a relationship is not the same as understanding its direction or its dynamics over time. This stage covers the tools that go beyond a single fitted equation — formal causality testing that is robust to integration order, rolling-window estimation that reveals how relationships evolve, and the error correction framework that ties short-run movements to a long-run equilibrium.

08 / Stage 3
Time Series Causality Tests — Toda-Yamamoto, Fourier & Quantile Variants

Granger causality testing is sensitive to the integration and cointegration properties of the data. This video demonstrates how to run several causality tests, including the robust Toda-Yamamoto approach that sidesteps pre-testing pitfalls, along with Fourier and quantile variants for breaks and distributional causality.

Stage 4

Volatility & Projection

The final stage moves from modelling the mean of a series to modelling its variance, and then to communicating results. Financial and high-frequency data routinely show volatility clustering that the mean models in earlier stages assume away. This stage closes the course with conditional volatility modelling and a tool for turning estimated relationships into clear, projectable visuals.

Everything up to here has modelled the conditional mean — what a series is expected to do on average. But in finance and energy data, the interesting action is often in the variance: quiet periods punctuated by bursts of turbulence. ARCH and GARCH models exist precisely to capture that, and they are the natural endpoint once the mean dynamics are well understood.
11 / Stage 4
Overview of ARCH–GARCH Models in Stata

When volatility clusters — large changes followed by large changes — the constant-variance assumption fails. This video explains the concepts behind ARCH and GARCH models and walks through estimating them in Stata to capture time-varying conditional volatility in financial and high-frequency series.

A note on sequencing

Stage 1 is a prerequisite, not a suggestion. If your series is not imported and declared correctly with tsset, the lag and difference operators that every later command relies on will either fail or silently mislead. If you have not settled the levels-versus-logs and stationarity questions, an ARDL bounds test in Stage 2 cannot be interpreted.

Stages 3 and 4 carry more flexibility depending on your research focus. If your interest is the direction of influence between variables, the causality and ECM videos are the core. If you work with financial or high-frequency data, the ARCH–GARCH material is where the real questions live. The stronger your foundation before specialising, the better your instincts will be when something goes wrong — and in time series, the trouble usually hides in the data preparation you were tempted to rush.

These methods are genuinely hard to use well. The places where they break — a missed unit root, an ignored break, an autocorrelated residual — are rarely obvious until a reviewer asks the question you should have asked yourself.
